# The Relocate Raleigh Guide

**A practical, family-first playbook for moving to the Triangle.**

## About This Guide

This is the guide we wish we'd had before we moved. No fluff, no tourism-board boosterism, no "top 10 reasons to love Raleigh" filler. Just the information you need to decide whether the Triangle is the right move for your family — and, if it is, how to execute it without learning every lesson the hard way.

You'll find:

- Honest comparisons between Raleigh and the expensive metros most transplants are leaving
- Neighborhood breakdowns written for families, not investors
- A scouting-trip itinerary that actually tells you what you need to know in 3–5 days
- The Wake County school system decoded (it's not like anywhere else)
- Financial realities — taxes, property tax, hidden costs
- Moving logistics, from cross-country movers to your first DMV trip
- The cultural infrastructure that makes the Triangle feel like home
- A seasonal survival guide for people moving from cooler, drier climates

Everything here reflects the Triangle as of 2026. Where things change fast — home prices, school boundaries, employer hybrid policies — we've noted the date so you know when to double-check.

> **A note on scope.** This guide is written for families relocating from higher-cost U.S. metros. The comparisons lean on Bay Area, Seattle, New York, Boston, DC, and Chicago numbers because that's where most of our readers are moving from. If you're coming from somewhere else, the neighborhood and logistics chapters still apply — the "what you're trading" math just looks different.

## Chapter 1 — Is Raleigh Right For You?

### The honest filter

Before we sell you on the Triangle, here's who it *isn't* a great fit for:

- People who need ocean or mountains out their back door. The coast is 2 hours. The mountains are 3–4. Both are drivable weekends; neither is a Tuesday afternoon.
- People allergic to humidity. July and August are real.
- People who want a walkable, transit-dependent lifestyle at NYC scale. Downtown Raleigh and parts of Durham are walkable. Most of the region isn't.
- People who want the exact same cultural density as a Tier-1 coastal metro. The Triangle has more than most outsiders realize, but less than what you're leaving.

If any of those are dealbreakers, save yourself the flight and stop here. If they're tradeoffs you can live with, keep reading.

### Quick-orientation map

The Triangle is really three cities plus a handful of suburbs wrapped around Research Triangle Park in the middle. From the outside in:

- **Raleigh** — the state capital. Has a downtown, but most of "Raleigh" is sprawling neighborhoods inside and outside the 540 beltway.
- **Durham** — smaller, artsier, home to Duke. Southern Durham blends into RTP.
- **Chapel Hill** — college town, home to UNC. Small, expensive, academically intense.
- **Cary / Apex / Morrisville** — the "western suburbs." Tech-family heavy, great schools, newer housing.
- **Holly Springs / Fuquay-Varina / Garner** — southern suburbs. Cheaper, more space, longer commutes.
- **Wake Forest / North Raleigh** — northern suburbs. Established, family-oriented.

## Chapter 3 — The Scouting Trip

You get one, maybe two trips before you decide. Don't waste them.

### Before you book

- **Don't come in late March through April.** Pollen season is real and you'll form a negative impression that doesn't reflect the other eleven months.
- **Do come on a weekday.** You need to experience the RTP commute at rush hour. A Saturday visit tells you nothing about the Tuesday you'll actually live.
- **Fly into RDU.** Rent a car. There is no "get around by transit" version of this trip.

### The 3-day itinerary (for decisive families)

**Day 1 — Orient and eat.** Land midday. Drive through RTP on your way to your rental. Dinner in downtown Durham (American Tobacco Campus area) — it's the most distinctive restaurant scene in the region. Start with the place most different from where you're leaving.

**Day 2 — Neighborhoods and schools.** Two-neighborhood day. Drive the streets on a Tuesday morning. Walk around a strip center. Eat lunch at the grocery store (seriously — it tells you who lives there). If you have kids, drive past the assigned elementary school at drop-off. This is the day that decides it.

**Day 3 — The non-work life test.** Morning at Umstead State Park or Jordan Lake. Lunch at an international market (Grand Asia Market in Cary is the canonical one — the food court is excellent). Afternoon: downtown Raleigh or the North Hills area. This answers "what will weekends be like?"

### Home-buying timeline from a high-COL market

The order of operations when you're selling a $1.8M house in the Bay Area to buy an $850K house in Cary is not obvious. Common paths:

- **Sell first, rent here for 6–12 months, then buy.** Safer, lets you neighborhood-shop in person.
- **Bridge loan / HELOC against current home.** Faster, more expensive, more risk.
- **Contingent offer.** Usually not competitive in either market.

### Hidden costs nobody mentions

- HOA fees (common in newer neighborhoods, sometimes substantial)
- Summer HVAC electricity bills
- Car inspection and emissions (annual, cheap, easy to forget)
- Lawn care (St. Augustine / Bermuda / fescue — yard maintenance is a thing here)
- Humidity-related home maintenance (mold, HVAC capacity)

## Chapter 6 — Logistics and the First 90 Days

### Choosing a moving approach

Three tiers, ordered most-to-least expensive:

1. **Full-service national mover.** They pack, load, drive, unload, unpack. Expensive; the least work.
2. **Container / POD.** You pack, they deliver and drive. Middle cost, middle effort.
3. **DIY truck (U-Pack, Penske, Budget).** Cheapest, most work, often not worth it for a family household.

## Chapter 8 — Seasonal Survival Guide

### Spring: the pollen reckoning

Late March through mid-April, everything — cars, porches, dogs, you — turns yellow-green. Pine pollen is the visible culprit; tree pollen is the one that actually wrecks your sinuses. If anyone in your family has seasonal allergies, get an allergist appointment set up before spring.

### Summer: humidity and storms

> *[RESEARCH: typical summer temp and humidity, the afternoon thunderstorm pattern, hurricane risk inland (Triangle is 2.5 hours from coast), what past hurricanes like Florence, Fran, and Helene did to the region.]*

### Fall: the payoff

October and November are the months that sell the region. Cool mornings, warm afternoons, long golden light, football Saturdays. This is when you'll be glad you moved.

### Winter: ice, not snow

Triangle winters are mild by most standards — highs often in the 50s — punctuated by occasional ice storms that shut down the region for 24–72 hours. The problem isn't the amount of ice; it's that the infrastructure isn't built for it, so trucks don't run, power goes out, and schools close on a forecast.

> *[RESEARCH: typical ice-storm frequency, what to have on hand, generator considerations.]*

### Home and yard implications

- Central air is non-negotiable, and your unit will work hard June–September. Budget for replacement before you think you need to.
- Yard work is part of life here. Most homes have lawns; most HOAs have standards.
- Mold and mildew are a thing. Check crawl spaces and basements before you buy.
